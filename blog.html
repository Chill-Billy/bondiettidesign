The Rooster-Bear of Beartooth Pass



This photo was composed by asking MidJourney AI to imagine the following: "Rooster singing a ballad accompanied by a banjo playing bear on a windswept plain with a river running through it."



There are tales from times past that tell of a giant demi-god who roamed the rocky mountains north of what is now called Yellowstone.


The creature's height equaled that of a coastal cedar. Its apparance was that of a large, beaked grizzly bear whose fur turned to feathers and back again before your very eyes. Its feet were not bear paws, but rooster's claws with a massive razor sharp barb at each heel. It strummed a magical lute which it used to control the weather. When you heard the thundering in the mountiains, you knew the great Rooster-Bear was calling forth the rains.


What most folks don't know, was that the melodies and cadence were one and the same of an American singer called John Denver. In fact, if you listen closely, you can almost hear the plucked guitar patterns of 'Sunshine On My Shoulders' in the pitter-patter of the last rain drops of an afternoon storm in the Rocky Mountains.

The Mystical Cat Goddess of the Bountiful Harvest



This photo was composed by asking MidJourney AI to imagine the following: "A female black cat with greenish yellow eyes whose tails spiral in a golden ratio floating kindly above a windswept plain."



"Meow"


The meow isn't the sound of the average house-cat. It echoes and reverberates through the air, hovering above the fields of wheat. It makes you want to hug your loved ones and pounce on spiders. You know this call. It is the season of the harvest and you know that you must drop all plans and commence the harvest immediately.


In one region of the lands of the northern plains, a poor, kind wheat-farmer took in a small kitten who had lost her mother. They did not know this kitten was actually the trickster-god, Meg ~ the god of bountiful harvests.


Years ago, Meg grew bored of taking care of the people of the lands, and so took for herself two decades out of her rule of the catdom to see what it was like to be taken care of. She took a kitten's form and decided to test the loyalty of one man named Billbro Braggins. Billbro took Meg in, of course, and he proved to be a loyal companion. He scritched her behind the ears and under the chin exactly the way she liked and baked up many fine treats that rivaled the taste of fresh killed field-mouse.


Meg lived a full and happy cat's life, having had many litters of kittens and spending her days hunting small creatures among the wheat and barley fields and snatching butterflies from her roof-top perch. She decided to move back to the spirit lands, but as a reward for a life well lived, she crept into her servant-master's dreams and thanked him for his service. She left her friend with the following words:


"Billbro, I must move on! I am leaving you with a blessing and a call to action whenever you hear my voice on the winds. This is the day you must harvest the fields. Do exactly as I say and your family will be blessed."


Meg was gone in the morning. Farmer Billbro knew this was no ordinary dream, and so made his mind up to follow her orders. Years later, one September, Billbro heard Meg's call. It echoed like a thunder-clap, but was very distinctly a 'meow'. He at once called to his community and told them of his dream. The elders of the community were planning to wait another two weeks to let some of the greener fields turn gold, but Billbro took note of the cloud formations and implored the elders to heed Meg's call.


The elders of the village discussed the facts amonst themselves for the morning. They remembered the farm cat's magnificently lustrous fur and her otherworldly, glowing, green and gold eyes. Sometimes these eyes betrayed a god's intelligence. They finally assesed Billbro's past deeds, and since he had lived a life of honor and kindness, the elders saw no reason not to deem him a worthy and true-prophet, and after all, had they also not heard the clear sound of a cat's meow echoing across the hilltops?


All of the fields were harvested, dried and gleaned two weeks early. The tribe had lost a small portion of its crops to the early harvest. Just when the elders were beginning to question their sanity, and the neighbor villages fields were looking more glorious than any previous years, a 100 year storm blew in. It rained, and rained, and rained for three months. The rain storms lasted so long, they formed new lakes and carved new river beds, Many low-land villages were swallowed up in floods.


When the other villages were dashing to save their fields, the Braggins tribe was protecting their food-stores and digging canals around thier village. The storm became like a monsoon season that lasted through winter, and the rain turned to sleet and then to snow. The tribe dug in for a long winter and were saved through to spring by their early harvest. Most other tribes in the region were no more. The Braggins tribe re-populated the lands and told the legend of Meg for centuries.


To this day, all high plains children are taught to be extra super kind to cats, and the region is known for its bountiful harvests.
